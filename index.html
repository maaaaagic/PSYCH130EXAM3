Topic Reference

DSM-5-TR Criteria

  • Obsessions and/or compulsions present.
  • Obsessions = intrusive, persistent thoughts, urges, or images.
  • Person attempts to suppress or neutralize the thoughts.
  • Compulsions = repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed in response to obsessions.
  • Behavior aims to reduce distress or prevent feared events.
  • Time-consuming (>1 hour/day) or cause impairment.

Common Obsessions

  • Contamination
  • Responsibility for harm
  • Violence or morality
  • Religion
  • Symmetry or order

Common Compulsions

  • Cleaning/decontamination
  • Checking
  • Repeating actions
  • Ordering or arranging
  • Mental rituals

Epidemiology

  • Lifetime prevalence ≈ 1.3%
  • Slightly more common in women
  • Typical onset: childhood or adolescence
  • Often chronic without treatment
  • High comorbidity with depression and anxiety

Risk Factors / Etiology

  • Heritability ≈ .40–.50
  • Serotonin involvement
  • CSTC brain circuit dysfunction
  • Cognitive distortions (inflated responsibility, thought-action fusion)
  • Stressful life events may trigger onset

Treatment

  • First-line: Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)
  • Exposure to feared stimuli while preventing rituals
  • SSRIs commonly prescribed
  • Clomipramine sometimes used
  • ~69–75% show significant improvement

Key Differential

  • vs BDD – BDD obsessions restricted to appearance
  • vs GAD – GAD involves generalized worry
  • vs OCPD – OCPD traits are ego-syntonic
  • vs ASD – repetitive behaviors for routine/sensory regulation
  • vs Tic disorders – tics not driven by obsessions
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Preoccupation with perceived appearance defects.
  • Defects appear minor or unobservable to others.
  • Repetitive behaviors such as mirror checking or reassurance seeking.
  • Concerns not limited to weight or body fat.
  • Often occupies 3–8 hours per day.

Epidemiology

  • Lifetime prevalence ≈ 3%
  • Slightly more common in women
  • Typical onset: adolescence
  • ~33% meet criteria for OCD

Risk Factors

  • Heritability similar to OCD (~.40–.50)
  • Detail-focused visual processing bias
  • Overvaluation of physical attractiveness
  • Appearance-related teasing or criticism
  • Cultural emphasis on attractiveness

Treatment

  • Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)
  • SSRIs frequently used
  • Cosmetic surgery rarely improves symptoms

Differential Diagnosis

  • vs OCD – OCD obsessions broader themes
  • vs Eating disorders – focus on weight/shape
  • vs Social anxiety – fear of embarrassment
  • vs Delusional disorder – fixed beliefs
DSM-5-TR Criteria
  • Difficulty discarding possessions regardless of value
  • Strong perceived need to save items
  • Distress when discarding
  • Accumulation clutters living spaces

Epidemiology

  • Lifetime prevalence ≈ 1.5%
  • Gender roughly equal
  • Often begins in childhood
  • Symptoms worsen with age

Risk Factors

  • Heritability ≈ .40–.50
  • Cognitive deficits in organization
  • Decision-making difficulty
  • Emotional attachment to objects

Treatment

  • Modified ERP focused on discarding
  • Organizational skills training
  • In-home decluttering interventions
  • Medication less effective than for OCD

Differential Diagnosis

  • vs OCD – OCD hoarding driven by obsessions
  • vs Dementia – clutter due to cognitive decline
  • vs Depression – clutter from low motivation
  • vs Collecting – collectors organize items
